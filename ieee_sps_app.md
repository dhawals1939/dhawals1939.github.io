## 3. Your Interests

**word limit:** 500 words

My interest in signal processing grew from an unconventional path. My earlier background was in software engineering, computer vision, and real-time graphics. During my bachelor’s and master’s training, I mostly encountered image processing through software tools and off-the-shelf pipelines. I could use these tools, but I did not yet deeply understand the mathematical and physical principles that made them work.

That changed during my PhD at Dartmouth. In computational photography lectures with my advisor, I first began to understand the Fourier domain as more than an abstract mathematical topic. I saw how choosing the right signal representation can significantly reduce algorithmic complexity, for example by expressing shift-invariant kernels in a Fourier basis. I was also fascinated by how CT reconstruction follows from the Fourier slice theorem. These ideas changed my view of imaging: instead of treating images as arrays processed by software, I began to see imaging as a problem of signal formation, transformation, measurement, and reconstruction.

My first PhD project made this shift practical. The project, accepted to IEEE TPAMI, involved acousto-optic imaging using programmable sound waves in water. I had to learn waveform generation, synchronization, triggering, and acousto-optic physics on a steep timeline. By driving carefully designed sound signals through water, we could control the refractive index and create optical line profiles for scanning. This removed the need for mechanical scanning components and their inertia, enabling extremely high-speed imaging. The project showed me how signal synthesis and physical optics can combine to create new sensing capabilities.

I later contributed to an underwater backscatter communication project by helping build an end-to-end waveform generation and hardware synchronization pipeline. This work involved timing delays, triggers, Nyquist sampling, phase modulation, and phase-shift keying. It helped me understand communication signals not only as equations, but as physical signals that must be generated, transmitted, measured, and decoded under real hardware constraints.

My interest deepened further through a LiDAR project based on ideas from communication hardware. I learned how a message can be mixed with a carrier for transmission and then demodulated to recover information, and how this principle supports software-defined radio. Working with quadrature modulation and demodulation helped me understand how communication-style signal processing could be adapted to time-of-flight sensing. By combining these ideas with LiDAR hardware, we were able to sample depth at extremely high rates and reduce the noise floor enough to recover vibrations from surfaces using a laser spot.

I believe I deserve to be selected because signal processing has become central to both my research and my future career. My path taught me that disciplinary boundaries should not become constraints. If I had remained only within software engineering or graphics, I would have missed the powerful ideas that signal processing provides for building real imaging, communication, and sensing systems. I want to use these ideas to design systems that see faster, deeper, and more robustly than conventional cameras.

## 4. Personal Statement
 
**word limit:** 500 words

In the next five years, I see myself completing my PhD and establishing a research profile in computational imaging, LiDAR, and signal-processing-driven sensing systems. I want my doctoral work to show that signal processing is not only something applied after data is captured, but something that can shape the entire sensing pipeline: the waveform, the physical modulation, the sensor measurement, and the reconstruction algorithm. My immediate goal is to publish strong research that combines hardware, physics, and computation to recover useful information from difficult optical measurements.

My current internship at Bosch Research in Pittsburgh is also shaping this direction. I am working on building a sensing system for an under-skin/subsurface imaging project, where useful signals are weak, distorted, and affected by tissue scattering. This experience connects directly to my academic interest in computational imaging through scattering media and has strengthened my goal of building sensing systems that work outside ideal laboratory conditions.

In ten years, I hope to be leading research either as a faculty member or as a researcher in an advanced R&D environment. I want to build imaging and sensing systems that combine model-based signal processing, machine learning, optics, and hardware design. My specific interests include high-speed LiDAR, acousto-optic scanning, optical communication, event-based sensing, and imaging through scattering media. In all of these areas, the central challenge is signal processing: how to design measurements, handle noise, exploit structure, and recover information reliably from imperfect data.

In twenty years, I hope to lead a research group that develops new ways to sense the physical world beyond the limits of conventional cameras. I want to build systems capable of million-frame-per-second scanning, high-rate underwater optical communication, precise interferometric depth measurement, and imaging in difficult media such as tissue or turbid water. These goals require a deep understanding of sampling, modulation, demodulation, reconstruction, inference, coherence, noise, and physical signal propagation.

Signal processing will remain the foundation of this future career. It gives me the language to connect software with physics. It explains why coherence matters, how a carrier can encode a message, how phase can carry precise information, how sampling affects what can be recovered, and how noise can be reduced through better measurement design. It also gives me tools to combine classical methods with machine learning in a principled way.

I also want to share this perspective with students. Because I came from a software and graphics background, I understand how easy it is to treat signal processing as a distant or difficult field. I want to mentor students who may not initially see themselves as signal-processing researchers and help them realize how these tools can transform imaging, communication, robotics, and scientific measurement.
